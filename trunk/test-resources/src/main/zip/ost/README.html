The Östersundom model

The Östersundom model represents a larger district containing of 14 areas, each with different square meters amounts of multi-storey buildings, town houses, smaller residential buildings, public buildings (schools, kindergarten etc.), commercial buildings and shops. When it comes to energy solution, the whole Östersundom area contains of one combined heat and power (CHP) plant. This plant is controlled according to the heat demand while producing electricity at the same time. There is a centralized thermal storage for the whole district while thermal storage capacity can be added to each area separately. The size of the storages is modified through their volumes. The Östersundom district model also contains a district heating component from which one could change the supply and return temperatures of the district heating network and the lower heating value of the fuel used in the CHP. It can be regarded that this district heating component is forming a unity with the CHP component. See Figure 1 for overview of the Östersundom model. Following are the component descriptions.

NOTE. There might a limit for some of the user-given values, exceeding these might cause problems with summations since the Östersundom model contains components with thermodynamic properties that might cause the simulation to crash once they become unstable.

The urban areas

Each (urban) area of the district is composed of the following components: energy demand of six different building types, solar collectors, solar panels, a connection pipe to the district heating network and a heat storage tank.

The energy consumption of each specific area is mainly dictated by the floor area amounts of the different building types.

Solar energy production (heat and electrical) is dependent on the amount of collectors/panels installed, the coordinates (longitudinal, latitudinal) of the area. The output temperature of collectors and that of the tank or the district heating network has also an effect in the output of solar collectors. Solar energy is deducted from the demand before the excess production is stored/injected to the district heating network. Excess heat that could not be stored in tank is injected to the district heating network and transferred to the central tank. In case the central tank is fully loaded, the excess heat is lost.  Heat can be added to the district heating network as long as the local temperature is below 120 °C.

The district pipes contribute to heat losses depending on their length, thickness and temperature difference between outdoor air temperature and temperature of the water flowing in the pipes. The length of each pipe is regarded to be the distance from the CHP to the certain district.

The tank of the area has the same properties as the central tank and more information about this can therefore be found from the Central storage section.

The CHP plant component

The combined heat and power (CHP) plant produces both heat and electricity for the energy demand of the Östersundom district. The component is wired to react to the heating demand of the area, which means that it will cover for all heating demand if properly adjusted. The balance between heat demand and heat produced can be calculated from the difference of the specific output component output values (se section below). The simulation does not fail of the production does not meet the demand but it can be regarded that the extra heat is needed from other sources unknown to the model.

Electricity is produced as a side-product and might not always match the demand and the lacking amount is considered to be delivered from the national grid. The proportion of Thermal power rating to Electrical power rating is recommended to be kept as 3:1 if a micro CHP is considered.

The CHP component is linked to the DH control component from which the CHP can be turned on or off and the lower heating value of the fuel used can be set.

The central storage component

Each urban area contains of a similar storage as the central storage. This storage is connected to the district heating network. Heat is extracted and added to the storage as long as the capacity allows for it and the storage temperature is within the boundaries set. The maximum capacity of the storage is decided by the boundary temperatures and the volume of the tank. Heat from solar thermal collectors is dependent on the temperature of the tank once the tank volume is bigger than zero; otherwise it is dependent on the temperature of the district heating return temperature of the area. Heat loss of the tank is dependent on the tank temperature, ambient temperature and insulation layers thickness. The insulation thickness is given as a ratio of the tank height which can be seen from one of the component properties. The central storage component was considered to be located next to the CHP plant. However, a storage located in any of the areas can be set larger than the central storage.

The adder components

There are several adder components in the Östersundom model used for summing together outputs from different components. The inputs and outputs are scalable in order to adjust to user preferences (unit, positive/negative). There are six adders included in the Östersundom model used for summing the following values from all of the fourteen areas:

·         Heat Balance (excess heat demand – excess heat production)

·         CHP Heat Demand (the heat demand given to CHP)

·         CHP Heat Produced (the amount of heat generated by the CHP plant)

·         Cooling Balance (cooling demand, no specific cooling production in the area)

·         Electricity Balance (electricity demand – electricity generated be CHP)

·         CHP Electricity Produced (electricity generated from heat production)
